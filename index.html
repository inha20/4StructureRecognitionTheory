Abstract

Abstract

Structure Recognition Theory (SRT) is a theoretical framework for understanding how humans discover structures, assign meaning to them, generate research questions, and initiate research programs. Originating from empirical observations of Karnaugh map visual patterns, symmetric Boolean functions, and variable rearrangement invariance, SRT proposes seven exploratory hypotheses (H1–H7) addressing the process from perceiving a difference, to attending to structure, to detecting explanatory significance, to formulating a research question.

The framework also models Human-AI collaborative discovery as a division of cognitive labor in which the human researcher detects explanatory significance and selects research directions, while the AI collaborator expands the explanation space and maintains continuity across sessions.

All hypotheses are treated as exploratory propositions — conceptual models and research directions rather than established conclusions.

Core Research Question

How do humans detect explanatory significance?

This question evolved through a sequence of increasingly abstract formulations, each built on the empirical work of the preceding stage:

  • 1
    Initial "Why does this checkerboard appear in the Karnaugh map?"
  • 2
    Intermediate "Why do humans recognize patterns as structures?"
  • 3
    Later "Why do humans discover structures?"
  • 4
    Current "Why do certain structures feel worth investigating?"

This sequence is part of the research record. It documents not a logical reconstruction but the actual historical development of the research question.

Research Generation Framework

SRT proposes that research emerges through a structured sequence of cognitive transitions. Each step is a necessary precondition for the next:

Difference Something is noticed as unusual or unexpected
↓
Attention The unusual element receives sustained focus
↓
Structure A pattern is recognized as a structure with properties
↓
Meaning The structure is assigned significance beyond itself
↓
Research Worthiness An implicit filter asks: "Is this worth investigating?"
↓
Question A research question is formulated
↓
Research A research program is initiated

The Seven Hypotheses (H1–H7)

All hypotheses are exploratory. None should be presented as established conclusions. They are conceptual models — working propositions that direct further inquiry.

H1

Structure Discoverer Hypothesis

Human advantage in research lies primarily in structure discovery, not in computation or retrieval.

Exploratory
H2

Representation Transformation Hypothesis

Different representations of the same phenomenon reveal different structural properties. Structures can be hidden or disclosed by choice of representation.

Supported by observations
H3

Attention Filter Hypothesis

Not all patterns receive investigation. An attention filter selects which differences become structures.

Exploratory
H4

Significance Filter Hypothesis

An implicit filter evaluates whether a structure is "worth investigating" — prior to explicit reasoning.

Exploratory
H5

Explanation Anticipation Hypothesis

Humans anticipate that an explanation exists before finding it. This anticipation drives investigation.

Exploratory
H6

Explanatory Significance Hypothesis

Research programs begin not from anomalies alone, but from detecting explanatory potential — the sense that a structure points toward something explainable.

Exploratory
H7

Mature Structure Discoverer Hypothesis

The core human role in research is detecting explanation opportunities — recognizing that a structure has an explanation worth finding. (Mature revision of H1.)

Exploratory

Concept Evolution Framework

Alongside the Research Generation Framework, SRT proposes a Concept Evolution cycle describing how the concepts used to perceive phenomena themselves evolve through research:

Phenomenon A concrete observable event (e.g. the checkerboard pattern)
↓
Concept A named abstraction capturing the pattern (e.g. "structural invariance")
↓
Expanded Perception The concept functions as a lens — new observations become possible
↓
New Observation Previously invisible phenomena become visible
↓
New Concept A refined or extended concept emerges (cycle continues)

The two frameworks — Research Generation and Concept Evolution — are proposed to interact as part of a unified research generation process. Concept formation enables new questions; new questions enable concept revision.

Empirical Basis — Papers 1–3

Papers 1–3 provide the empirical foundation. Each paper's core observation connects to specific hypotheses in SRT.

Empirical · Paper 1

KMap Structure Invariance

Checkerboard patterns recognized immediately in XOR/XNOR Karnaugh maps before formal analysis.

Connects to: H2 (representation reveals structure), H5 (explanation anticipated)

Empirical · Paper 2

Symmetric Boolean Functions

Hamming Weight layer patterns perceived before the Layer structure was formally computed.

Connects to: H1 (structure discovered before computation), H2

Empirical · Paper 3

Variable Rearrangement Invariance

Invariance sought and recognized across 24 variable arrangements; equivalence classes formulated.

Connects to: H4 (invariance as significance criterion), H6 (explanatory potential)
