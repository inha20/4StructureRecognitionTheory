## Program-Level Central Question

> **How do humans come to see something new?**

This question frames the entire research program at its highest level of abstraction.  
It subsumes the theory-level central question:

> **How do humans detect explanatory significance?**

Both questions remain open. The second is the current operational focus of Paper 4.

---

## The Question Hierarchy

The questions form a layered structure from empirical observation (Level 0)  
through structure and discovery (Levels 1–2), significance and questions (Levels 3–4),  
explanation (Level 5), and concept evolution (Levels 6–8).

Each level asks a different kind of "why" — the levels do not merely add detail;  
they shift the type of question being asked.

---

### Level 0 — Observation

*What empirical phenomena require explanation?*

These are the ground-level observations that started the research program.  
They are factual starting points, not questions about psychology or cognition.

- **Q0.1** Why do XOR/XNOR Boolean functions produce checkerboard patterns in Karnaugh maps?
- **Q0.2** Why do symmetric Boolean functions generate recurring visual forms (ring, corner, point) in K-map representations?
- **Q0.3** Why does variable rearrangement produce visually distinct K-maps for the same logical function?
- **Q0.4** What structural properties of a Boolean function survive all 24 variable arrangements?

*Primary case studies: Papers 1, 2, 3*  
*Connected hypotheses: H2 (representation reveals structure)*  
*Note: Level 0 questions have candidate answers in the empirical papers. They serve as the foundation that motivates all higher-level questions.*

---

### Level 1 — Structure

*How do patterns become structures in human perception?*

Level 0 describes what happens in mathematics. Level 1 asks what happens in the observer.

- **Q1.1** Why do humans recognize these patterns as *structures* rather than noise or coincidence?
- **Q1.2** What distinguishes a *pattern* from a *structure* in human perception?
- **Q1.3** Why is the checkerboard recognized as a single unified whole rather than a cell-by-cell aggregate?
- **Q1.4** Does structure recognition require prior conceptual knowledge, or can it operate without it?
- **Q1.5** Is structure recognition a perceptual act, a cognitive act, or both?

*Connected hypotheses: H1 (structure discoverer), H2 (representation transformation)*  
*Connected open problems: OP-01 (attention selection), OP-04 (representation design)*  
*Relation to Level 0: Level 1 does not ask why the pattern exists (Level 0), but why humans notice and name it as a structure.*

---

### Level 2 — Discovery

*Why do humans discover particular structures at particular times?*

Level 1 asks how patterns become structures. Level 2 asks why some structures are discovered and others are not.

- **Q2.1** Why do humans discover particular structures and not others of equivalent visual complexity?
- **Q2.2** What determines which structures attract sustained investigative attention?
- **Q2.3** Is structure discovery a deliberate act of search, or a spontaneous recognition?
- **Q2.4** What role does prior conceptual knowledge play in enabling structure discovery?
- **Q2.5** If AI and a human examine the same Karnaugh map, do they discover the same structures?

*Connected hypotheses: H1 (structure discoverer), H3 (attention filter)*  
*Connected open problems: OP-01 (attention selection), OP-06 (AI structure discovery)*  
*Relation to Level 1: Level 2 is not asking how patterns become structures, but why particular structures are selected for sustained attention.*

---

### Level 3 — Significance

*Why do certain structures feel important?*

Level 2 asks which structures attract attention. Level 3 asks why some attended structures feel worth investigating.

- **Q3.1** Why do certain structures feel *important* while others of equal clarity do not?
- **Q3.2** What are the operative criteria of the significance filter (H4)?
- **Q3.3** Is invariance under transformation a universal significance criterion, or is it domain-specific?
- **Q3.4** Does the significance judgment operate consciously, pre-reflectively, or both?
- **Q3.5** Can the significance filter be made explicit and taught as a skill?
- **Q3.6** Does the significance filter differ across researchers, or are there shared criteria?

*Connected hypotheses: H4 (significance filter), H6 (explanatory significance), H7 (mature structure discoverer)*  
*Connected open problems: OP-02 (significance boundary), OP-05 (invariance significance)*  
*Relation to Level 2: Attention (Level 2) is a prior selection; significance (Level 3) is a further evaluation. A structure can attract attention without being deemed research-worthy.*

---

### Level 4 — Question Generation

*How do structures become research questions?*

Not all significant structures generate research questions. Level 4 asks about the transition from significance judgment to question formation.

- **Q4.1** Why do certain structures develop into research questions while others do not?
- **Q4.2** What distinguishes a structure that generates curiosity from one that does not?
- **Q4.3** Is research question generation a skill that can be trained and improved?
- **Q4.4** Does the research question originate in the structure, in the observer, or in their interaction?
- **Q4.5** What is the role of conceptual vocabulary in making a question formulable?
- **Q4.6** Can a question only be formed after the right concept is available?

*Connected hypotheses: H4, H6, H7*  
*Connected open problems: OP-02 (significance boundary), OP-08 (research question origin)*  
*Relation to Level 3: A structure can pass the significance filter without generating a specific research question. Question generation requires not just judgment of significance, but the formation of a specific inquiry.*

---

### Level 5 — Explanation

*How do humans orient toward explanation before finding it?*

Level 4 describes question formation. Level 5 asks about the anticipatory structure of the inquiry — the sense that an explanation exists before it is found.

- **Q5.1** How do humans detect *explanatory significance* — the sense that a structure should have a structural explanation?
- **Q5.2** Do humans anticipate the existence of an explanation before they discover it?
- **Q5.3** What triggers the sense that "there must be a reason for this"?
- **Q5.4** Is explanation anticipation a separate cognitive act from structure recognition?
- **Q5.5** Can explanation anticipation occur without any prior theory about the domain?
- **Q5.6** Is explanation anticipation necessary for research initiation, or merely facilitative?

*Connected hypotheses: H5 (explanation anticipation), H6 (explanatory significance)*  
*Connected open problems: OP-03 (explanation anticipation mechanism)*  
*Relation to Level 4: Question generation (Level 4) describes what the question is. Level 5 describes the motivational orientation — the felt sense that the structure demands an explanation — that drives the inquiry.*

---

### Level 6 — Concept

*How does the formation of new concepts change what can be observed?*

*(Level 6–8 from CoreQuestions_vNext.md, integrated with SRT v0.2 H8–H10)*

The levels above address what happens when a human encounters a phenomenon.  
Levels 6–8 address how *the existence of a concept* changes what can be encountered in the first place.

- **Q6.1** What makes a concept effective as an observation lens (H8)?
- **Q6.2** Are concepts discovered or invented?
- **Q6.3** Why do some concepts generate new research programs while others remain merely descriptive?
- **Q6.4** Can a concept be deliberately designed to maximize its structure-revealing power?
- **Q6.5** Is the visibility-enhancing power of a concept (H8) measurable?

*Connected hypotheses: H8 (concept as lens), H10 (generative concept)*  
*Connected open problems: OP-07 (concept-as-lens)*  
*Relation to Level 5: Levels 0–5 describe the processing of a single encounter with a phenomenon. Level 6 asks how having certain concepts changes what encounters are possible.*

---

### Level 7 — Expanded Perception

*How does concept acquisition change what is visible?*

Level 6 asks about the concept as a lens. Level 7 asks about the change in perception that results from acquiring the lens.

- **Q7.1** How do concepts change what a researcher can observe?
- **Q7.2** Why do some concepts reveal previously invisible structures?
- **Q7.3** Can new observations be produced by conceptual change alone, without new data or new instruments?
- **Q7.4** What is the relationship between conceptual change and observation change — do they always co-occur, or can one precede the other?
- **Q7.5** Is there a limit to how much a single concept can expand perception?

*Connected hypotheses: H8 (concept as lens), H9 (concept evolution)*  
*Connected open problems: OP-07 (concept-as-lens)*  
*Evidence from this program: The concept of "structural invariance" (available after Paper 3) made the question "what properties survive transformation?" formulable — a question that was not available before the concept existed.*

---

### Level 8 — New Discovery

*How does the concept evolution cycle generate new discovery cycles?*

Level 7 describes the change in perception due to a concept. Level 8 asks about the systemic dynamics — how a concept, by enabling new perception, generates new research, which generates new concepts.

- **Q8.1** What causes a research field or program to expand over time?
- **Q8.2** How do concepts generate further concepts through expanded perception?
- **Q8.3** Can the Concept Evolution Cycle (H9) be modeled formally?
- **Q8.4** Can the cycle be used deliberately — can a researcher accelerate their own discovery cycle?
- **Q8.5** Are there structural conditions under which the concept evolution cycle stalls or terminates?

*Connected hypotheses: H9 (concept evolution), H10 (generative concept)*  
*Evidence from this program: Pattern → Layer Structure → Equivalence → Structural Invariance → Structure Recognition Theory → Human-AI Collaboration Model. Each concept made the next visible.*

## Program History as Question Evolution

The research program's central question has evolved through successive refinements.  
This evolution is itself an example of the Concept Evolution Cycle (H9):

| Stage | Central Question | Conceptual Enabler |
|---|---|---|
| Initial | Why does this checkerboard appear? | Karnaugh map as visualization tool |
| Early | Why do humans recognize patterns as structures? | Distinction between *pattern* and *structure* |
| Middle | Why do humans discover structures? | Concept of *discovery* as distinct from *recognition* |
| Later | Why do certain structures feel worth investigating? | Concept of *significance filter* (H4) |
| Current | How do humans detect explanatory significance? | Concept of *explanatory significance* (H6) |
| Program-level | How do humans come to see something new? | Concept of *concept as lens* (H8) |

**What this table shows:** Each new central question required a new concept to become formulable.  
This is not incidental — it is the Concept Evolution Cycle demonstrating itself in the research program's own history.
